The history of post-Gettier epistemology is largely the history of proposed fourth conditions, each elegant, each defeated by a new counterexample.</p><p><strong>No false lemmas.</strong> Look again at Smith: his belief was inferred through a falsehood ("Jones will get the job"). So perhaps the fix is to require that the justification rest on <em>no false premises</em> — no false "lemmas," in the mathematician's sense. This handles Smith neatly. But it founders on the fake barns. Henry infers nothing false; he simply looks at a real barn and believes, truly and on the basis of normal vision, that it is one. There is no false lemma anywhere in his reasoning, and yet the verdict still stands: not knowledge. The luck is environmental, not inferential, and no premise-cleaning will catch it.</p><p><strong>Defeasibility theory.</strong> Keith Lehrer and Thomas Paxson proposed in 1969 that knowledge is <em>undefeated</em> justified true belief: there must be no true proposition that, were the believer to learn it, would destroy their justification. In the sheep case, the truth "the white shape you see is a dog" is a <em>defeater</em> — learning it would collapse your warrant. Genuine knowledge has no such lurking defeater. The challenge is distinguishing genuine defeaters from misleading ones; the theory requires delicate machinery to avoid ruling out perfectly good knowledge.</p><p><strong>The causal theory.</strong> Goldman, in his 1967 paper "A Causal Theory of Knowing," suggested that knowing requires the fact that makes your belief true to be <em>causally connected</em> to the belief itself. Smith fails because the coins in his own pocket played no causal role in producing his belief. This is a beautiful idea for perception and memory — but it strains for mathematics and general truths, which have no obvious causal contact with our minds, and the fake barns slip through again, since the real barn <em>does</em> cause Henry's belief in the ordinary way.</p><p><strong>Reliabilism.</strong> Goldman shifted ground in 1979 ("What Is Justified Belief?"): perhaps what matters is that the belief was produced by a <em>reliable cognitive process</em> — one that yields truths more often than falsehoods. Vision is reliable; wishful thinking is not. This externalist turn was hugely influential, requiring nothing the believer can introspect.
