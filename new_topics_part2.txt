When you obey a moral law because you recognize it as one reason demands, you are not submitting to anything external; you are obeying yourself at your most rational. Its opposite is <strong>heteronomy</strong>: letting your will be determined by something outside reason — desire, fear, the promise of pleasure, the command of another. An action done for reward, however correct, is heteronomous, and so without moral worth.</p><p>From autonomy flows Kant's most resonant concept: <strong>dignity</strong> (<em>Würde</em>). Things that serve our inclinations have a <em>price</em> — they can be exchanged for equivalents. But a rational being, an end in itself, has no price. It has dignity, which is "beyond all price" and admits of no equivalent. This is why persons may never be reduced to mere instruments: you cannot put a person on the same ledger as a commodity. Much of modern human-rights language descends, often unknowingly, from this single Kantian thought.</p><p>The system has a precise structure. Kant divides duties along two axes — to self and to others, perfect and imperfect — yielding the four cases. <strong>Perfect duties</strong> admit no exceptions in the agent's favor and forbid specific acts (do not lie, do not coerce). <strong>Imperfect duties</strong> require adopting general ends (develop yourself, help others) but grant discretion in execution. Standing behind all of it is a startling metaphysical claim: morality presupposes <strong>freedom</strong>. If we were merely natural mechanisms, links in the causal chain, "ought" would be meaningless. So Kant locates moral agency in the <strong>noumenal self</strong> — the self considered as a thing-in-itself, outside the deterministic order of appearances — even though we can never theoretically prove such a self exists. Freedom is a necessary presupposition of practical reason, not an object of knowledge.</p><p>This is why Kant's ethics is the great rival to <strong>consequentialism</strong>, the family of views (later perfected by the utilitarians) holding that an act's rightness lies entirely in its results.
