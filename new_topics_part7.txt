        body: `<p>To handle the problem with care, it helps to name its parts. The hidden premise Hume exposes is now called the <strong>Principle of the Uniformity of Nature</strong> (PUN): roughly, that the regularities observed in the parts of nature we have sampled hold also in the parts we have not — across time, across space, in the unobserved as in the observed. Almost every inference we trust smuggles it in.</p><p>The pattern of inference itself is <strong>enumerative induction</strong>: from "all observed F's have been G" we conclude "all F's are G," or at least "the next F will be G." All observed emeralds have been green; therefore emeralds are green. All observed ravens have been black; therefore the next raven will be black. The conclusion always says more than the premises — it reaches beyond the sample to the whole population, beyond the past to the future. That surplus is exactly what no observation can underwrite.</p><p>It is crucial to separate two claims that Hume runs close together. The <strong>descriptive thesis</strong> is psychological: it describes <em>how we in fact reason</em> — by habit, by the mind's projection of expectation after repeated exposure. The <strong>normative skeptical thesis</strong> is epistemological: it asks <em>whether we are justified</em> in reasoning this way, and answers that we have no rational ground. Hume the naturalist describes the mechanism; Hume the skeptic denies it a foundation. Much of the literature since is an attempt to find a justification that Hume declared impossible — or to argue that none is needed.</p><p>The most influential twentieth-century response refuses the demand for justification altogether. <strong>Karl Popper</strong>, in <em>Logik der Forschung</em> (1934, later <em>The Logic of Scientific Discovery</em>), accepted Hume's argument as valid: induction has no rational basis, and he meant it. But he believed science does not actually proceed by induction. His escape exploits a deep <strong>logical asymmetry</strong>. No number of confirming instances can prove a universal law — a thousand black ravens do not establish that <em>all</em> ravens are black — yet a single counterexample <em>refutes</em> it outright. One white raven, and the universal claim is dead. The asymmetry is the asymmetry of the logical rule <em>modus tollens</em>: if a theory predicts P and we observe not-P, the theory is false, with full deductive force and no induction required.</p><p>From this Popper built <strong>falsificationism</strong>. Science advances not by accumulating confirmations but by bold <em>conjecture</em> followed by ruthless attempts at <em>refutation</em>. A theory is scientific not because it can be proven but because it can be <em>tested</em> — because it forbids something, sticks out its neck, risks being wrong. We never know that a surviving theory is true; we know only that it has not yet been falsified. Knowledge grows by elimination, like evolution by the death of the unfit. Whether this truly evades Hume, or merely relocates the problem — for don't we still <em>trust</em> theories that have survived testing, and isn't that trust itself an inductive bet? — is one of the deepest disputes in the philosophy of science.</p>`

        body: `<p>Just when it seemed the problem might be tamed, the American philosopher <strong>Nelson Goodman</strong> uncovered a second, subtler trap — the <em>new riddle of induction</em>, set out in <em>Fact, Fiction, and Forecast</em> (1955). Hume had asked <em>why</em> we are entitled to project the observed onto the unobserved. Goodman showed that even if we grant ourselves the right to project, we face a prior catastrophe: deciding <em>what</em> to project. For the very same evidence confirms incompatible predictions equally well.</p><p>He invents a predicate. Call an object <strong>grue</strong> if it is examined before some future time <em>t</em> and found green, or else (if not examined before <em>t</em>) is blue. Now consider every emerald observed up to today. Each one is green — and therefore each one is also <em>grue</em>, since each was examined before <em>t</em> and found green. So our total evidence confirms "all emeralds are green" and "all emeralds are grue" to exactly the same degree. Yet the two diverge violently about the future: the first predicts the next emerald, examined after <em>t</em>, will be green; the second predicts it will be <em>blue</em>. Identical evidence, opposite forecasts.</p><p>The point is devastating in its precision. The <strong>syntactic form</strong> of enumerative induction — "all observed F's are G, so all F's are G" — cannot by itself tell us which predicates are legitimate. Plug in "green" and you get sane science; plug in "grue," with the identical logical shape, and you get nonsense. So the form <em>underdetermines</em> the content. Something beyond the bare pattern of inference must single out the <strong>projectible</strong> predicates — the ones fit to carry an inductive conclusion — from the unprojectible ones. Goodman's own answer was <em>entrenchment</em>: "green" has a long history of successful use in our practices, "grue" does not. But this seems to describe our preference rather than vindicate it, and the search for a deeper criterion of natural, projectible kinds remains open. One might object that "grue" is defined by reference to a time and is therefore artificial — but the grue-speaker turns the charge around: in <em>their</em> vocabulary, where "grue" and "bleen" are basic, it is <em>our</em> "green" that must be defined using a time. The symmetry is the sting.</p><p>The dominant modern framework for thinking about all this is <strong>Bayesian</strong> confirmation theory, which treats degrees of belief as probabilities and updates them by <strong>conditionalization</strong> on new evidence via Bayes's theorem:</p><pre>P(H | E) = P(E | H) · P(H) / P(E)</pre><p>Here the <em>posterior</em> probability of a hypothesis H given evidence E rises as the evidence is more expected under H than otherwise. Repeated confirming instances drive the posterior upward, and a family of <em>convergence theorems</em> shows that agents who start with different priors will, under broad conditions, come to agree as evidence accumulates. This is elegant, and it captures much of scientific practice. But it does not slay Hume. The update rule takes the <em>prior</em> P(H) as given, and nothing in the calculus dictates what the priors should be. A grue-believer and a green-believer can each be perfectly coherent, each obey Bayes's theorem flawlessly, and reach opposite conclusions — because they began with different priors. The Bayesian framework, critics charge, does not solve the problem of induction so much as <strong>relocate</strong> it into the choice of priors, where it sits as stubbornly as ever.</p>`

        body: `<p>Three centuries on, the problem of induction is not a museum piece. It is alive in philosophy seminars, in the foundations of statistics, and — startlingly — at the heart of modern machine learning. Several lines of response mark the contemporary frontier, none of them decisive, all of them illuminating.</p><p><strong>Reichenbach's pragmatic vindication.</strong> Hans Reichenbach, in <em>Experience and Prediction</em> (1938), conceded that induction cannot be <em>justified</em> — we cannot show it will work. But he argued it can be <em>vindicated</em>: we can show that <em>if any method works at all</em>, induction will work too. Suppose nature has some stable limiting frequency to be discovered; then the inductive policy of projecting observed frequencies is guaranteed to converge on it in the long run, while no rival method carries the same guarantee. Induction is the rational bet not because we know nature is uniform, but because if she is uniform in any discoverable way, induction is bound to find it. The standard worry: infinitely many "inductive" rules converge in the limit while disagreeing wildly in the short run — the very run we actually live in.</p><p><strong>Strawson's dissolution.</strong> P. F. Strawson, in <em>Introduction to Logical Theory</em> (1952), argued that the demand itself is confused. To ask whether induction <em>as a whole</em> is reasonable is like asking whether the law is legal: the question misfires. To call a belief reasonable, in matters of fact, <em>just means</em> that it is well supported by inductive evidence. Induction does not need an external license from some grander tribunal; it is partly constitutive of what "reasonable" means. Asking induction to be justified by something <em>other</em> than itself is asking it to be deductive, which it is not and was never meant to be. Critics reply that this saves the word "reasonable" while leaving Hume's real worry — will it actually <em>work</em>? — untouched.</p><p><strong>Reliabilism and externalism.</strong> A different move, descending from the externalist epistemology of Alvin Goldman and others, locates justification not in an argument the believer can give but in the actual reliability of the process. If induction is in fact a reliable belief-forming process — if it does in fact tend to produce true beliefs — then inductive beliefs are justified, whether or not we can step outside the process to prove its reliability without circularity. This trades Hume's demand for a non-circular proof for the more modest claim that justification does not require one. Whether that is a solution or a redefinition of the target is exactly what is at issue.</p><p><strong>The no-free-lunch theorems.</strong> Most striking is a formal echo of Hume from the mathematics of machine learning. The <em>no-free-lunch</em> theorems of David Wolpert and William Macready (1996–97) prove that, averaged over <em>all</em> possible target functions, no learning algorithm outperforms any other — including random guessing. A learner can generalize from training data to unseen cases only by assuming a prior over which patterns are likely: its <em>inductive bias</em>. With no bias, no generalization; with a bias, you have simply chosen which uniformities to bet on, exactly as Hume's reasoner must assume the future resembles the past, and exactly as Goodman's projectibility problem demands a choice among predicates. Every neural network that generalizes does so on borrowed faith. The mathematics has rediscovered the philosopher's predicament: <em>there is no learning without a prior commitment that learning cannot itself justify.</em></p><p>So the question Hume posed in 1739 stands open, and the stakes have only grown. We have built a civilization — and now a generation of artificial minds — on inductive inference, on the wager that the unobserved will resemble the observed. The wager keeps paying. We still cannot say why we are entitled to make it. Perhaps, as the naturalists hold, induction is simply what minds do, no more in need of a license than breathing; perhaps, as the optimists hope, some vindication will one day close the circle. Or perhaps the deepest lesson is Hume's own quiet one: that the most basic engine of all knowledge is something we practice flawlessly and ground not at all — that reason, at its root, runs on a faith reason cannot supply.</p>`
