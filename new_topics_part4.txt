What do they share? Not color, not shape, not sound. Yet we possess the concept of perfect Equality, against which we judge the sticks deficient. The realm of Becoming is the sensible world — temporal, plural, perishable, forever sliding from one state to another, never fully <em>is</em> anything. The realm of Being is the Forms — eternal, singular, immutable, perfectly themselves. Knowledge, Plato insists, can only be of what truly is; of what is always becoming and never resting, there can be only opinion. This is why the senses can never deliver knowledge: their objects will not hold still long enough to be known.</p><p>The chief argument <em>for</em> positing Forms is the <strong>one-over-many</strong>. Whenever many things share a common predicate — many large things, many just acts, many beds — Plato reasons that there must be a single Form, distinct from them all, in virtue of which they are so called. The Form is the one over the many: the unity that makes the plurality intelligible. Particulars stand to their Form by two relations Plato describes with deliberately metaphorical language: <strong>participation</strong> (<em>methexis</em>) — the particular "shares in" the Form — and <strong>imitation</strong> (<em>mimesis</em>) — the particular is a likeness or copy of the Form, as a portrait images its sitter.</p><p>At the summit sits the <strong>Form of the Good</strong>, the sun of the allegory. It is not merely the highest among the Forms; it is their source. As the sun makes things both visible and able to grow, the Good makes the Forms both knowable and able to be. Plato makes the most vertiginous claim in the <em>Republic</em>: the Good is not itself a being but lies "beyond being" — <em>epekeina tēs ousias</em> — surpassing even the Forms in dignity and power. From this single phrase a whole mystical tradition would later grow.</p><p>Scholars divide over how literally to read all this. The <strong>two-worlds interpretation</strong> takes Plato to posit two separate realms — a transcendent heaven of Forms and a separate physical world below — so that to know the Forms is to look away from the world entirely. The <strong>two-aspects (or two-levels) interpretation</strong> reads the Forms not as a separate place but as a deeper mode of the same reality: the Forms are what the world is, properly understood, rather than a second world alongside it.
